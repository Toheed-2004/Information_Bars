════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  UNDERSTANDING THIS COMPARISON
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Series A and B are information bars built from the same asset.
  They differ only in DATA SOURCE:
    A = calibrated and built from 1-minute OHLCV bars
    B = calibrated and built from raw tick (aggTrade) data
    C = Time 60min — fixed-interval TIME BARS (the null hypothesis baseline)

  TIME BAR BASELINE: A time bar closes every N minutes by the clock,
  regardless of market activity. It carries no information-sampling
  advantage. If A or B cannot beat C on most criteria, the information
  bar is not extracting structure the clock cannot already provide.

  DIFFERENT BAR COUNTS ARE NORMAL. The research question is quality,
  not quantity.  Quality = low serial correlation + high entropy +
  low kurtosis + uniform sizes + duration CV in adaptive range.
  A high TIMEOUT % means bars are closing by time limit, not by signal.

  SERIAL CORRELATION IN RETURNS  (the most important de Prado criterion)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
